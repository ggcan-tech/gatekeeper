# Litigation 101 — the founder's field guide (SDNY civil motions)

Verified against primary sources 2026-07-16 (SDNY rules, FRCP, IAALS studies).
Read twice before any litigator interview.

## The life of a motion (recite this)

Filing day: case opened, **judge assigned by lot the same day, public on the
docket** → service → day 21: defendant answers OR moves to dismiss → SDNY
gate: most judges require a **pre-motion letter** (+ often a conference)
BEFORE the motion may even be filed (2-6 weeks) → briefing: motion →
opposition 14 days → reply 7 days (Local Rule 6.1(b); usually stipulated to
~6-12 weeks total; word limits 8,750/8,750/3,500) → "fully submitted" →
**decision ON THE PAPERS, typically 2-8 months, no deadline** (only backstop:
the twice-yearly CJRA "six-month list") → appeal if dispositive.

## The five facts that shape our product

1. **The judge is known from day one.** Our report's timing works for the
   entire life of the case.
2. **~76% of summary-judgment motions get NO hearing** (IAALS ten-district
   study); SDNY's own guide: the court "will typically decide the motion
   based solely on the arguments in the papers." Oral argument is an
   appellate norm (21.9% of appeals; 2d Cir. 36%). **The brief IS the
   argument** — a moot court for the BRIEF is the right product; live
   oral-sparring tools target a moment that mostly doesn't exist at district
   level.
3. **Liman and Furman's own individual rules disfavor oral argument** (they
   grant it more readily only to give junior lawyers podium time). Our exam
   judges are paper judges.
4. **Associates write, partners argue and decide spend.** First draft:
   associate. Edit/sign/argue: partner (~1 hour reviewing vs ~8 drafting).
   The first careful reader in chambers is often a LAW CLERK writing a bench
   memo. Sell to the partner; the second chair is the daily user.
5. **The insertion moment:** substantially-complete draft, after the first
   partner turn, before cite-check lockdown — roughly 5-10 days pre-filing.
   (Pre-argument: the moot ritual slot, 1-3 weeks out.)

## Vocabulary (use these correctly, never their cousins)

movant (the side making the motion) · dispositive motion (can end the case:
MTD, MSJ) · pre-motion letter (SDNY permission step) · brief / memorandum of
law (the argument document; the "motion" is technically the request) ·
opposition · reply · sur-reply (extra brief, needs permission) · fully
submitted (briefing complete, judge's clock informally starts) · on the
papers (decided without hearing) · chambers (the judge's office + staff) ·
law clerk (judge's junior lawyer; writes bench memos) · bench memo (clerk's
pre-decision summary) · meet-and-confer (required opposing-counsel
negotiation before discovery motions) · second chair (the #2 lawyer on a
matter) · moot (practice argument with colleagues as judges) · cite-check
(final accuracy pass, locks the draft) · six-month list (public list of
motions pending >6 months — judges clear backlogs before it).

## Five things never to say (and what to say instead)

1. ✗ "We predict your judge" → ✓ "Court-calibrated rehearsal; the
   judge-specific edge is +7 points over a name-only baseline, disclosed."
2. ✗ "When's the hearing?" → ✓ "Most of these get decided on the papers,
   right?" (instant credibility)
3. ✗ "Your AI judge decides the case" → ✓ "It's a stress-test of the draft —
   preparation intelligence, not legal advice."
4. ✗ "Who's the defendant's judge?" → ✓ There is ONE judge per civil case,
   assigned at filing, both sides face the same one.
5. ✗ "We replace your associates" → ✓ "It's the moot your brief never gets —
   your associates' drafts, red-teamed before the partner's name goes on it."
